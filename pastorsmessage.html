Connecting - February 2024

Social and Emotional Learning

Welcome February! Our snowy winter is almost half-gone and we are soon to be well on the way to spring with Ash Wednesday on Valentine's Day and Easter on March 31st.

For the Lenten worship season, including Lenten classes and church missions, we will be continuing our response to the violent tragedy that happened at the Perry High School on January 4th as we raise our awareness about how we are called to act in the world.

One thing we hope to glean from scripture is how Christ responded to mental or spiritual unwellness. 2000 years ago, society was also responding, and Christ responded to these individuals and groups, often making grounded proclamations in regard to what he experienced. For nearly 2000 years in the Western world, the church has been seen by many as the greatest authority on social behavior, answering the question, How should I (or we) act in the world?

Now we have access to evidence-based learning that measures data on specific learning tools and practices, making evident the most helpful ways to keep individuals and society mentally and emotionally well in health, relationships, and ability to learn. There is evidence that shows that most who learn and live these new practiced examples can live more healthy, productive, and fulfilling lives. In recent years, most public schools have adopted this kind of training for teachers and curriculum for students. This helps students of all ages to grow strong and well in their minds, in communication, and in their emotions. Because of these best practices, classrooms are calmer and students can enjoy a better learning environment which they have generously given to one another.

We will soon have the opportunity to use these new evidenced based tools because we will be trained by the public-school approved curriculum, The Character Effect, that will be enhanced with scripture for Lent in our church. The focus of the worship messages and training will be to teach social and emotional learning (SEL).

Some of the vocabulary when talking about mental and emotional wellness can be triggering to some. The meaning of words like mental illness or mental disease or prevention keep changing in different settings or in kinds of unwellness and the terminology becomes offensive. The power and grace of newer, wiser words can be chosen uniquely for different situations, and not used as broadly as they have been used in the past. We can learn these new more specified words for unique conditions.

When my granddaughter was teaching SEL in her school, she took attendance by asking the students for an emotional check-in when they entered. To learn about their body's messaging system, they were given a long list of emotions to choose from to expand the variability of the way they could interpret their own emotions. Humans often interpret their emotions in limited ways. People only use a few words, saying they are happy, sad, mad, afraid, angry, surprised, or disgusted. How many more emotions can you name? 20? 50? What about joy or exhaustion? Awe or amusement? By learning to identify more emotions and figuring out how they present in our bodies, we can gain more emotional intelligence and can recognize them more easily in others.

By developing better communication with our bodies and minds, we can learn to modulate our emotions so we can be more regulated by not getting stuck in our emotions that are meant to be fluid. For example, as I preach about how important it is for Christians to continue to be witnesses and teachers in the community by being socially and emotionally healthier, I will probably get butterflies from the anxiety of sharing new information. But I will try to see my body's presentation of raised heart rate and tightened muscles not as anxiety, but as feelings of excitement, so I can get my butterflies to fly in formation! Then my anxiety can be released. This is just one SEL example that I have learned so far.

I look forward to this emotional sacred journey together with you during Lent.

Rev. Beth
